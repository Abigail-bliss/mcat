## Passage 10 (Questions 52–55)

D-Limonene is a terpene found in the peels of citrus fruit that holds promise as an anti-cancer agent. In the presence of oxygen, D-Limonene undergoes autoxidation by a free radical chain reaction, forming hydroperoxides. An experiment was designed to study the rate of oxygen absorption by D-limonene with changing hydroperoxide concentration. The amount of gas produced was measured with a gas burette, and the hydroperoxide content was determined by iodometric titration with sodium thiosulfate. In this procedure, hydroperoxide is reacted with iodide ion and starch for 15 minutes before titrating with thiosulfate (S₂O₃²⁻). Complexed iodine and starch is blue in solution. The structure of D-limonene and the titration reactions are shown below.

**Figure 1** — Structure of D-Limonene. *A cyclohexene ring with a methyl group (H₃C–) on the ring and an isopropenyl group (C(CH₃)=CH₂) substituent; one ring double bond.*

> **Reaction 1:** ROOH + 2H⁺ + 2I⁻ → I₂ + ROH + H₂O
>
> **Reaction 2:** I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻

ROOH represents the hydroperoxide product from the autoxidation of D-limonene. The reaction data were used to generate Figure 2.

**Figure 2** — Oxygen absorption and hydroperoxide concentration. *Dual y-axis vs. Time (hrs, 0–12): left = Meq. RO₂H present (0–10), right = Oxygen absorbed (mL/hr, 0–100). Both O₂ absorption (filled squares) and Meq. RO₂H present (open squares) rise sigmoidally over time; O₂ absorption leads and plateaus near ~90 mL/hr around 8–10 hrs.*

Further tests of oxygen absorption rate were performed in the presence of acid and base. The results are shown in Figure 3.

**Figure 3** — O₂ absorption in the presence of acid and base. *Oxygen absorbed (mL, 0–500) vs. Time (hrs, 0–16) for: 1% sulfuric acid, 1% phosphoric acid, 1% sodium carbonate, 1% citric acid, and Water. 1% sodium carbonate (base) shows by far the greatest O₂ absorption; the acids suppress absorption (lowest curves).*

## Questions

### Question 52 ✗ (missed)

In Reaction 2, iodine is titrated with thiosulfate. What color change signals the endpoint?

- *Subtopic 664 — Indicators · Topic 175 — Titration · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. Green to blue
- B. Blue to green
- **C. Blue to clear ✓ (correct)**
- D. Clear to blue — **✗ (her answer)**

**Solution:** Iodide ion and starch were both added back in Reaction 1, so starch is already in solution at the start of Reaction 2. Iodine (I₂) and starch complex to form a blue solution, and Reaction 2 shows I₂ is present at the start of the titration. Therefore the solution starts **blue** and turns **clear** as I₂ is converted to iodide ion (endpoint) → **Choice C**. Answer A (green→blue) and Answer B (blue→green) give the wrong colors. **Her trap (D):** "clear to blue" *reverses* the actual direction — the blue complex is consumed, not formed, during the titration. *Starch–I₂ = blue; titrating away the I₂ = blue → colorless.*

### Question 53 ✗ (missed)

Suppose researchers need a purified sample of D-limonene with minimal contamination. To accomplish this, D-limonene could be doubly distilled:

- *Subtopic 687 — Distillation · Topic 178 — Separations and Purifications · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 5: Organic Chemistry*

- **A. under a nitrogen atmosphere. ✓ (correct)**
- B. below atmospheric pressure.
- C. under UV light.
- D. using filtered, purified air. — **✗ (her answer)**

**Solution:** Distilling under nitrogen excludes air — and therefore oxygen — from reacting with the limonene. Because the passage's main premise is that D-limonene undergoes automatic oxidation in the presence of oxygen, excluding oxygen is a logical requirement to obtain a pure sample → **Choice A** (an oxygen-free distilled sample). Answers B, C, and D don't exclude air. **Her trap (D):** "filtered, purified air" still *contains oxygen* — filtering removes particulates, not O₂, so autoxidation would continue. *Tie the purification method back to the passage's degradation mechanism (O₂-driven autoxidation) → inert atmosphere.*

### Question 54 ✗ (missed)

The autoxidation of D-limonene in the presence of O₂ results in a mixture of products. Which structure is LEAST likely to be detected as an oxidation product of D-limonene?

- *Subtopic 677 — Structural formulas for molecules involving H, C, N, O, F, S, P, Si, Cl · Topic 176 — Covalent Bond · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 5: Organic Chemistry*

- A. Limonene ring bearing a **ketone** (C=O on the ring), isopropenyl intact — formula C₁₀H₁₄O.
- B. Limonene ring bearing a **hydroxyl** (HO–), isopropenyl intact — formula C₁₀H₁₆O.
- **C. Limonene ring bearing a **carboxylic acid** (HOOC–), isopropenyl intact — formula C₁₁H₁₆O₂. ✓ (correct)**
- D. Limonene ring bearing an **epoxide** (bridging O), isopropenyl intact — formula C₁₀H₁₆O. — **✗ (her answer)**

**Solution:** The autoxidation reaction includes limonene (C₁₀H₁₆) and molecular oxygen as the only reactants. The formulas are: A = C₁₀H₁₄O, B = C₁₀H₁₆O, C = C₁₁H₁₆O₂, D = C₁₀H₁₆O. **Choice C increases the number of carbons from 10 to 11**, but there is no additional carbon source among the reactants — so C cannot be an autoxidation product, making it the correct answer to this **LEAST** question. Answers A, B, and D all keep 10 carbons; the gain of oxygen (A, B, D) and loss of 2 H (A) are consistent with oxidation. **Her trap (D):** the epoxide *is* a plausible oxidation product (adds only O, keeps C₁₀) — it's this a **negative-qualifier ("LEAST")** question, so the "normal-looking" oxidation product is a distractor, not the answer. *Always underline LEAST/EXCEPT/NOT and count atoms — the odd carbon count (C₁₁) is the tell.*
