## Passage 6 (Questions 29–33)

The primary glucose sensor is pancreatic glucokinase (GCK), an isozyme of hexokinase. GCK activity stimulates insulin secretion from the pancreatic β-cells in order to maintain blood glucose homeostasis. GCK is allosterically regulated and exhibits moderate cooperativity with glucose, but GCK is not inhibited by glucose-6-phosphate. Unlike other hexokinases, GCK is not oligomeric. The glucose cooperativity observed in GCK is unusual for a monomer with a single ligand binding site.

Two theories have been proposed to explain GCK cooperativity. Theory 1 proposes two separate catalytic cycles arising from two different conformational states. Theory 2 proposes one active state, with retention of the active state for a short period of time after catalytic turnover.

To examine these two theories, human GCK was selectively labeled at the Ile17 and Trp3 side chains and then examined using NMR. Two-dimensional NMR spectra (simultaneous ¹H and ¹³C) were collected in three states: unliganded, glucose-bound, and glucose and AMP-PNP bound. The researchers concluded that the GCK small domain is intrinsically disordered. Binding of glucose results in the GCK small domain becoming ordered. Once the enzyme product is formed, the ordered state of GCK is retained for a short period before it returns to its intrinsically disordered state.

Hypoglycemic hyperinsulinemia (HH) is a condition characterized by severely low blood glucose levels due to excess insulin. One cause of HH is a quadruple mutant GCK variant. This mutation stabilizes the α13 helix in an ordered state, resulting in reduced GCK cooperativity and increased affinity for glucose. Kinetic data for wild-type GCK and the α13-GCK variant are shown in Figure 1.

The α13-GCK variant and WT GCK + activator show hyperbolic (non-cooperative) curves that rise steeply at low glucose; plain WT GCK shows a sigmoidal (cooperative) curve, reaching similar Vmax (~2 µM G6P/s) only at higher glucose.*

**Q29 — monomer vs. oligomer → quaternary structure (from her annotation)**

- **Monomer:** a single polypeptide chain that functions as a complete, independent protein.
- **Oligomer:** two or more polypeptide chains (subunits) non-covalently associated to form the functional protein. Most hexokinase isozymes are oligomers.
- **Quaternary structure** is *defined as* the spatial arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits. A monomer has only one chain, so there is nothing to arrange — **no quaternary structure by definition.**
- Reasoning chain: "GCK is not oligomeric" (passage) → GCK is a monomer → only 1 chain → quaternary structure requires ≥2 chains → GCK **lacks quaternary structure** → Choice B.
- Bonus: this is also why cooperativity is unusual in GCK. Cooperativity normally comes from subunit–subunit communication (e.g., hemoglobin's 4 chains). GCK has one chain + one binding site, yet still cooperates — that's the puzzle the passage studies.
