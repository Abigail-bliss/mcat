### Question 20 ✗ (missed)

During a GC analysis performed using a polar stationary phase and an inert gas mobile phase, longer retention time is usually associated with:

- *Subtopic 688 — Chromatography: Column chromatography, gas-liquid chromatography · Topic 178 — Separations and Purifications · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 5: Organic Chemistry*

- A. decreased polarity. — **✗ (her answer)**
- **B. increased polarity. ✓ (correct)**
- C. decreased net charge.
- D. increased chirality.

**Solution:** Retention times in gas chromatography are mostly a function of the boiling point of the compounds being tested. However, the polarity of the stationary phase relative to the compound also matters: with a *polar* stationary phase, polar compounds interact more strongly with it. Further, compounds with increased polarity tend to have higher boiling points. The mobile phase is an inert gas, so more polar molecules spend more time interacting with the stationary phase → longer retention time (**Choice B**). **Her trap (A):** Answer A says the opposite — less polar compounds elute more quickly. Answer C is incorrect because decreased net charge would mean *less* interaction with a polar stationary phase and a lower boiling point. Answer D is false — retention times aren't related to chirality in standard GC (specialized chiral stationary phases exist, but no such circumstance is given). *Polar stationary phase + inert carrier gas ⇒ more polar analyte = longer retention.*

## Terms & Notes

### Passage — Biology/Chemistry Background

**{ anion free radical }**
A species that has both a negative charge (anion) and an unpaired electron (free radical). Here, phenol gains an extra electron via enzymatic reduction → becomes a radical anion (phenol•⁻). That radical then donates an electron to O₂, reducing it to superoxide (O₂•⁻, called "hyperoxide" in the passage) and regenerating neutral phenol — that's the "cycle" in redox cycling. The danger: superoxide + NO → peroxynitrite → toxic nitrated metabolites.

**{ in vitro and in vivo }**
- **In vitro** (Latin "in glass"): experiment done in a flask/test tube, outside a living organism. Here = Fenton's reagent reaction in a lab vessel.
- **In vivo** (Latin "in life"): experiment done inside a living organism. Here = dosing mice with ¹⁴C-benzene.
- Mnemonic: *vitro* → vial/vessel; *vivo* → vital/alive.

**{ free radicals }**
Atoms or molecules with at least one unpaired electron. Highly reactive — they grab electrons from nearby molecules, triggering chain damage (oxidative stress: DNA damage, lipid peroxidation, protein damage). Fenton's reagent (Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂) generates the highly reactive hydroxyl radical (•OH) by the Fenton reaction: Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → Fe³⁺ + OH⁻ + •OH. Common biological free radicals: •OH, O₂•⁻ (superoxide), NO•.

**{ diethylamine/nitric oxide (DEA-NO) | do i need to know what this is? }**
No. DEA-NO is an NO donor — a compound that releases nitric oxide (NO) as it breaks down. You don't need to know its structure or mechanism. The passage tells you all you need: it's the thing that adds exogenous NO to the Fenton reaction. When it's present (Figure 1B), the metabolite profile shifts — nitrobenzene and nitrophenol peaks shrink, hydroquinone (peak 9) grows — showing that NO changes which metabolites form. Treat DEA-NO as a black box: "adds NO."

### Q17 — Gas Chromatography Concepts

**{ gas-liquid chromatography (GLC / GC) | how does this work? }**
Sample is injected, vaporized, and swept through a long column by an inert carrier gas (mobile phase — helium or nitrogen). The column interior is coated with a liquid stationary phase. As compounds travel, they constantly partition between the gas phase and the liquid — more volatile compounds (lower boiling point, weaker attraction to the stationary phase) stay in the gas phase more and exit the column faster. Less volatile compounds linger in the liquid phase longer and exit later. A detector at the end records concentration over time → the chromatogram peaks. Bottom line: the column separates by volatility (= boiling point), and the carrier gas just moves everything through.

**{ GC retention time (tᵣ) | what is this? }**
The time from injection to when a compound's peak appears at the detector. Higher tᵣ = compound spent more time in the stationary phase = less volatile = **higher boiling point**. This is the rule you use to read Table 1: longer tᵣ → higher BP. (With a *polar* stationary phase there's an additional polarity component — more polar analytes also stick longer — but the boiling-point rule is the dominant one for this table.)

**{ cis,cis vs. trans,trans geometry | how do I reason about this myself? }**
- **Cis** = the two substituents are on the *same* side of the double bond (molecule bends). **Trans** = substituents on *opposite* sides (molecule extends).
- Muconaldehyde is OHC–CH=CH–CH=CH–CHO (six carbons, two double bonds, aldehyde on each end). "Cis,cis" means both double bonds are cis → the chain curves in on itself and can fold into a compact pseudo-6-membered ring. "Trans,trans" means both are trans → the chain stays long and linear.
- **How to reason about BP without memorizing the ring detail:** Linear molecules pack closer together → more intermolecular surface contact → stronger London dispersion (and dipole-dipole from the aldehydes) → higher boiling point. Compact/curved molecules can't stack as well → weaker forces → lower BP. Trans isomers are almost always more linear → almost always higher BP than their cis counterpart. (Same reason trans fats are solid at room temp — they pack into a tight crystal lattice; cis fats stay liquid.)
- **But on this question you didn't need the geometry at all:** tᵣ(*trans,trans*) = 8.05 min vs. tᵣ(*cis,cis*) = 7.85 min — read the answer straight from Table 1.

**Her trap — C (benzoquinone epoxide, tᵣ = 7.47 min):** This is actually the *shortest* retention time of all the compounds in the table — so the *lowest* boiling point of the options. The mistake was probably not scanning all the retention times before choosing. The fix: before picking the "highest BP" choice, glance at all four answer-choice tᵣ values and circle the max.
