### Question 10 ✗ (missed)

The average height of an American adult is 1.70 m. When an individual of this height is standing, by how much will the blood pressure at the bottom of the feet differ from the blood pressure at the top of the head? (Density_blood = 1060 kg/m³)

- *Subtopic 515 — Hydrostatic pressure (Pascal's Law, Hydrostatic pressure, P = ρgh) · Topic 155 — Fluids · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 6: Physics*

- A. 2000 Pa — **✗ (her answer)**
- B. 6000 Pa
- C. 10000 Pa
- **D. 18000 Pa ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** Pressure in a static fluid varies with depth: P = P_atm + ρgh. The pressure *difference* between feet and head is the ρgh term: ρgh = 1060 × 9.8 × 1.70 = 17,660 Pa ≈ **18,000 Pa (Choice D).** Approximating (1000 × 10 × 1.7 = 17,000 Pa) gets you to D just as fast. **Her trap (A, 2000 Pa):** likely a multiplication error / picking the wrong quantities. *Write P = ρgh, plug the three given numbers, estimate — don't eyeball.*

### Question 11 ✗ (missed)

Which change in solution composition would cause a protein to elute from a hydrophobic interaction column?

- *Subtopic 688 — Chromatography: Column chromatography, gas-liquid chromatography · Topic 178 — Separations and Purifications · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 5: Organic Chemistry*

- A. Decreasing pH
- B. Increasing pH
- **C. Decreasing salt concentration ✓ (correct)**
- D. Increasing salt concentration — **✗ (her answer)**

**Solution:** Hydrophobic interaction chromatography (HIC) relies on **high** salt to strengthen hydrophobic interactions between the protein and the column. **Decreasing** salt weakens those interactions, so the protein dissociates and elutes (Choice C). pH has no significant role in HIC (A and B out; charge neutralization is a small secondary effect at most). **Her trap (D):** increasing salt would *strengthen* binding — the opposite of eluting. *Key fact: HIC = "salt on to bind, salt off to elute" (the reverse of ion-exchange in feel).*

### Question 12

Students added HCl dropwise to a solution of phospholipids with an initial pH of 7.4. Upon reaching a pH of 4.0, what changes will have occurred to the [COO⁻] and [COOH] present on the phospholipid head?

- *Subtopic 652 — Weak acids and bases: dissociation of weak acids and bases with or without added salt · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. Increased [COO⁻], because water will abstract more protons.
- **B. Increased [COOH], because water will donate more protons. ✓ (correct — her answer)**
- C. Increased [COO⁻], because the [COOH] increased.
- D. Increased [COOH], because the [COO⁻] increased.

**Solution:** Apply Le Chatelier to RCOOH + H₂O → RCOO⁻ + H₃O⁺. Lowering the pH means [H₃O⁺] rises, shifting the dissociation equilibrium **left** (toward reactants), increasing the protonated carboxylic acid [COOH]. That proton can come from water or hydronium. A and C are wrong because [COO⁻] *decreases*, not increases. D is wrong because the acid and base cannot both increase simultaneously (and it makes the same reversed claim as C).

### Question 43 ✗ (missed)

The initial velocity V₀ of an enzyme catalyzed reaction at different substrate concentrations [S] is shown (V₀ vs. [S]: "No Inhibitor" and "+ Inhibitor" curves reach different V_max but ½V_max occurs at the same [S] = K_M for both, marked by a vertical dotted line at K_M). Which graph best represents the corresponding Lineweaver-Burke plot?

- *Subtopic 81 — Mixed Inhibition · Topic 22 — Control of Enzyme Activity · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 1: Biochemistry*

- A. Lineweaver-Burke plot (1/V₀ vs. 1/[S]) with two lines crossing the x-axis at *different* points (mixed inhibition — both K_M and V_max change).
- B. Lineweaver-Burke plot for a competitive inhibitor (lines share the same y-intercept, different x-intercepts). — **✗ (her answer)**
- C. Lineweaver-Burke plot for an uncompetitive inhibitor (parallel lines).
- **D. Lineweaver-Burke plot where both lines share the same x-intercept (same K_M), different y-intercepts. ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** The stem plot represents an enzyme inhibited by a pure **non-competitive inhibitor**, which does not change the K_M of the reaction. The vertical dotted line shows that ½V_max is reached at the same [S] for both the inhibited and non-inhibited reactions. So find the Lineweaver-Burke plot where both conditions have the same K_M (same point where the line crosses the x-axis) → **Choice D** (as seen by the x-intercepts of both lines, K_M does not change). Answer A is wrong: it illustrates *mixed* inhibition, with both K_M and V_max changing (mixed inhibition = inhibitor binds both free enzyme and enzyme-substrate complex). Answer B is the plot for a *competitive* inhibitor. Answer C represents an *uncompetitive* inhibitor. *Non-competitive: same x-intercept (K_M unchanged), V_max drops.*

### Question 44 ✗ (missed)

Tryptophan, shown below, has an intrinsic fluorescence that can be used to study protein folding. Why is this an effective method?

- *Subtopic 70 — Active Site Model · Topic 76 — Enzyme Structure and Function · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 1: Biochemistry*

- A. Free tryptophan decreases folding kinetics to measurable rates.
- B. Tryptophan acts as a chaperone to facilitate protein folding.
- C. Tryptophan fluorescence provides secondary structure formation during folding. — **✗ (her answer)**
- **D. The rate of folding can be calculated by the change in tryptophan fluorescence. ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** Folding kinetics can be calculated by the change in tryptophan fluorescence — generally, fluorescence *increases* as the protein folds, and this can be fit to a first-order rate expression to get a rate of folding → **Choice D**. Answer A is wrong: intrinsic fluorescence measures the tryptophan residues *within* the protein, not free tryptophan, and free Trp isn't known to alter folding rates. Answer B is wrong: tryptophan is not a known chaperone. **Her trap (C):** tryptophan fluorescence *reports on its local environment* (whether the residue is buried or solvent-exposed) but does not itself "provide"/create secondary structure — it gives no direct insight into secondary structure. *"Intrinsic fluorescence" = the Trp already in the protein; it's a readout (rate probe), not a folding participant.*

### Question 57 ✗ (missed)

Sanger sequencing is used to determine the nucleotide sequence of DNA. It requires the addition of dideoxyribose, which prevents additional growth of the nucleotide polymer. Which molecule is most likely used in Sanger sequencing?

- *Subtopic 1083 — General · Topic 31 — Recombinant DNA and Biotechnology · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles · Discipline 2: Biology*

- **A. Nucleotide with adenine base + triphosphate; sugar has H at both the 2′ and 3′ positions (dideoxyribose — no free OH). ✓ (correct)**
- B. Nucleotide with adenine base + phosphate; sugar has OH at both 2′ (HO) and 3′ (OH) positions (ribose).
- C. Nucleotide with adenine base + phosphate; sugar has HO and H (deoxyribose — retains a 3′-OH). — **✗ (her answer)**
- D. Nucleoside with adenine base; sugar has HO and H but **no phosphate group**.

**Solution:** Sanger sequencing works by using a DNA nucleotide *without a 3′ hydroxyl* to "poison" (terminate) the reaction. The normal DNA sugar is "deoxyribose"; this sugar lacking *either* a 2′ or a 3′ hydroxyl is "dideoxyribose" → **Choice A**. Answer B is wrong: that is a normal *ribose* ring (has 2′-OH). **Her trap (C):** that is a *deoxyribose* ring (retains the 3′-OH), which would still allow growth of the nucleotide chain — it's a normal dNTP, not a chain terminator. Answer D is wrong: there is no phosphate to allow attachment to the nucleotide chain, and it isn't dideoxyribose. *Chain terminator = missing the 3′-OH (dideoxy); confirm a triphosphate is present so it can still be incorporated.*
