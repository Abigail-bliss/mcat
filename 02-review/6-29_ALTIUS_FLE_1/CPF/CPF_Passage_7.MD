## Passage 7 (Questions 34–38)

Numerous mammalian enzymes utilize chromium in its non-toxic oxidation state, chromium(III), as a cofactor. Other chromium oxidation states, particularly chromium(VI), are highly toxic. Pollution can result in the introduction of toxic chromium(VI) to human water sources, or in the release of chemicals that convert chromium(III) to chromium(VI).

One method for removing chromium(VI) from water uses scrap iron to chemically reduce chromium to an oxidation state that is more easily precipitated as a metal hydroxide. Fe(II) is a product of Reaction 1 and also a reactant in Reaction 2, which runs simultaneously as Fe(II) is produced.

> **Reaction 1:** 2Cr₂O₇²⁻(aq) + 6Fe(s) + 28H⁺(aq) → 4Cr³⁺(aq) + 6Fe²⁺(aq) + 14H₂O(l)
>
> **Reaction 2:** Cr₂O₇²⁻(aq) + 6Fe²⁺(aq) + 14H⁺(aq) → 2Cr³⁺(aq) + 6Fe³⁺(aq) + 7H₂O(l)

The design of the apparatus used for this procedure is depicted in Figure 1. Peristaltic pumps are used to transport the fluids. The untreated water has a Cr(VI) concentration of 25 mg/L and a pH of 2.5.

**Figure 1** — Schematic of a chromium(VI) removal apparatus using scrap iron. *A vertical "Scrap Iron" column with sample ports P1 (bottom), P2 (middle), P3 (top); Cr(VI) inlet enters at the bottom, flows up through the scrap iron. Outflow goes to a second vessel where NaOH is added, then "To Clarifier."*

As the separation process proceeds, a residue slowly accumulates on the scrap iron surface and eventually reduces overall efficiency. Samples are taken periodically at ports P1, P2 and P3 to measure efficiency over time. Following the reduction step, a co-precipitation is performed under basic conditions. Solids and liquids are then separated at the clarifier. The concentration of Cr(VI) and Cr(III) at sample ports P1, P2 and P3, during a 216-hour water treatment trial are presented in Figure 2.

**Figure 2** — Concentration of Cr(VI) and Cr(III) at sample ports P1, P2 and P3 over 216 hours of operation. *Cr concentration (mg/L) vs. Time (h). At P3 (last port), Cr(VI) stays at 0 until ~48 h, then rises; Cr(III) at P3 rises over time. At P1/P2, Cr(VI) climbs and Cr(III) falls as the scrap iron loses efficiency. Curves for Cr(III) at P1/P2/P3 and Cr(VI) at P1/P2/P3 are plotted separately.*

## Questions

### Question 34 ✗ (missed)

For how many hours after startup did the system convert 100% of the Cr(VI) to Cr(III)?

- *Subtopic 642 — Oxidation number (common oxidizing and reducing agents, disproportionation reactions) · Topic 171 — Stoichiometry · SIR 4: Data-Based and Statistical Reasoning · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. 12 — **✗ (her answer)**
- B. 24
- **C. 48 ✓ (correct)**
- D. 100

**Solution:** The key is to recognize that the P1 and P2 measurements in Figure 2 can be *ignored* — the question asks about conversion by the system *as a whole*, and P3 is the last port in the system (Figure 1), so it gives the best approximation of overall conversion. Figure 2 shows that starting at 48 hours, some Cr(VI) is detected at P3; prior to that, 100% of Cr(VI) had been converted to Cr(III) → **48 hours (Choice C)**. **Her trap (A, 12):** at 12 hr the Cr(VI) concentration at P3 is still zero — but so is it at 24 hr (B); the *last* hour of full conversion is 48. Answer D (100) can result from reading the graph incorrectly — one of the values approaches 25 mg/L, and 100 is a number from the passage used as a distractor. *Read conversion off the final port (P3), not the intermediate ports.*

### Question 35 ✓ (correct)

If the apparatus in Figure 1 is operating optimally, which two precipitates should be present at the clarifier?

- *Subtopic 662 — Complex ions and solubility · Topic 174 — Solubility · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. Cr(OH)₃ and Cr(OH)₆
- B. Cr(OH)₃ and Cr(OH)₂
- **C. Cr(OH)₃ and Fe(OH)₃ ✓ (correct — her answer)**
- D. Fe(OH)₃ and Fe₂(Cr₂O₇)₃

**Solution:** These compounds result from the products of Reaction 2 — Cr³⁺ and Fe³⁺ — reacting with hydroxide (added as NaOH) to form a precipitate. If both precipitates are being formed, that indicates Reaction 2 is proceeding and the apparatus is accomplishing its designed purpose (reduce Cr(VI) → Cr(III)) → **Choice C**. Answer A is wrong: the passage states the oxyanions of Cr(VI) are highly soluble. Answer B includes Cr(II), which is never mentioned in the passage and does not form. Answer D includes dichromate, which is the *starting material* the apparatus is designed to reduce — it would not be present at the point of precipitation when the device is under optimal operation.

### Question 36 ✗ (missed)

Which chemical reduction occurs during Reaction 2?

- *Subtopic 642 — Oxidation number (common oxidizing and reducing agents, disproportionation reactions) · Topic 171 — Stoichiometry · SIR 5: (CAR SIR 1: Foundations of Comprehension) · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. Fe reduces Cr³⁺
- B. Fe³⁺ reduces Cr₂O₇²⁻ — **✗ (her answer)**
- **C. Fe²⁺ reduces Cr₂O₇²⁻ ✓ (correct)**
- D. Cr₂O₇²⁻ reduces Cr³⁺

**Solution:** Fe²⁺ gets oxidized to form Fe³⁺ in Reaction 2, so it is the *reducing agent* — it reduces the dichromate (Cr goes from Cr⁶⁺ in Cr₂O₇²⁻ to Cr³⁺). Six moles of Fe²⁺ reduce two moles of chromium from Cr⁶⁺ to Cr³⁺ → **Choice C**. (Recognizing this requires calculating the oxidation state of Cr in Cr₂O₇²⁻ = +6.) Answer A describes the reduction in Reaction 1, not Reaction 2. **Her trap (B):** Fe³⁺ is not a *reactant* in either reaction, so it cannot reduce anything (it's a product). Answer D is wrong: Cr₂O₇²⁻ is a strong *oxidant* and is shown acting as a reducing agent — backwards.

### Question 37 ✓ (correct)

During the first 48 hours, the pH of the reaction mixture is sampled as it passes from P1 to P3. Which pattern is expected?

- *Subtopic 649 — Ionization of water: definition of pH, pH of pure water · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- **A. pH will steadily increase ✓ (correct — her answer)**
- B. pH will steadily decrease
- C. pH will equilibrate to 7.0
- D. pH will remain constant

**Solution:** Because Reactions 1 and 2 both *consume* H⁺ ions, the reaction mixture must increase in pH (become less acidic) over time (pH = −log[H⁺]) → **Choice A**. Figure 2 shows that during the first 48 hours all Cr(VI) is being reduced to Cr(III); the consumption of H⁺ (per Reactions 1 and 2) decreases acidity and increases pH. Answer B states the opposite. Answer C is impossible — Reactions 1 and 2 require acidic conditions, so they couldn't proceed appreciably in a neutral solution. Answer D can't be true: pH cannot remain unchanged if H⁺ is being consumed.

### Question 38 ✗ (missed)

Based on Reactions 1 and 2, researchers interested in increasing the efficiency of the chromium(VI) removal process should add which compound to the untreated water?

- *Subtopic 642 — Oxidation number (common oxidizing and reducing agents, disproportionation reactions) · Topic 171 — Stoichiometry · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. NaOH
- **B. HCl ✓ (correct)**
- C. HClO₄ — **✗ (her answer)**
- D. KMnO₄

**Solution:** Both reactions require *acidic* conditions and consume H⁺, so adding an acid boosts efficiency. Answer A (NaOH) would *increase* pH — counterproductive. **Her trap (C, HClO₄):** perchloric acid *is* an acid, but ClO₄⁻ (like MnO₄⁻ in D) is a strong *oxidizer* — it would counteract a process designed to *reduce* chromium(VI). Adding **HCl (Choice B)** increases acidity and supplies additional H⁺ for Reactions 1 and 2 without introducing an oxidizer. Answers C and D are both strong oxidizers → wrong. *When picking an acid to add, screen out any whose anion is a strong oxidant (ClO₄⁻, MnO₄⁻) in a reduction process.*

- [ ] _(candidate) **Reducing agent = the species oxidized** — in a redox reaction, the reactant whose oxidation state goes UP is the reducing agent (Fe²⁺→Fe³⁺ reduces Cr₂O₇²⁻). Only reactants can reduce/oxidize, not products. (From Q36.)_
- [ ] _(candidate) **Choosing an acid without adding an oxidizer** — to acidify a reduction process, avoid acids whose anion is a strong oxidant (HClO₄→ClO₄⁻, KMnO₄→MnO₄⁻); use HCl. (From Q38.)_
- [ ] _(candidate) **Read whole-system output at the final port** — for "overall conversion," read the last port (P3) and ignore intermediate ports P1/P2. (From Q34.)_
- [ ] _(candidate) **Spotting strong oxidizing anions by oxidation state** — a polyatomic anion is a strong oxidizer when its central atom sits near its highest possible oxidation state (electron-starved → pulls electrons in). Classic list: MnO₄⁻ (Mn +7), Cr₂O₇²⁻/CrO₄²⁻ (Cr +6), ClO₄⁻/ClO₃⁻/ClO⁻ (Cl +7/+5/+1), NO₃⁻ (N +5, esp. concentrated/hot). Contrast: Cl⁻ in HCl is Cl at −1 (its lowest state) → not an oxidizer. Test: calculate the central atom's oxidation state (O = −2, solve for the rest), then ask if it's near the top of that element's range. (From Q38.)_
