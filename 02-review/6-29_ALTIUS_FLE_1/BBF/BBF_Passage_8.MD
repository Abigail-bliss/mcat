## Passage 8 (Questions 45–48)

_Neisseria meningitidis_ is the primary causative agent of bacterial meningitis. _N. meningitidis_ expresses a protein,
NhhA, which localizes to the bacterial membrane. This protein is necessary for infectivity and has been detected on the
membrane of all virulent strains. NhhA can also trigger apoptosis in macrophages when the bacteria is engulfed,
resulting in leakage of macrophage cell contents and damage to surrounding tissue. The NhhA protein forms trimers via
C-terminus binding of NhhA monomers. Trimer formation is necessary for the protein to translocate to the bacterial
membrane. A point mutation at the C-terminus of NhhA disrupts trimerization, reducing both infection rate and macrophage
apoptosis.

To investigate the effects of NhhA on apoptosis, a macrophage cell line was incubated with recombinant NhhA protein at
increasing concentrations (Figure 1) or with 400 nM NhhA for increasing amounts of time (Figure 2). The amount of
apoptosis was measured using a standard fluorescent assay.

> dose-dependently from ~1% (0 nM) to ~20% (400 nM); significant (\*) at 10, 40, 100, and 400 nM._

**Figure 2** — Apoptosis levels over a 20-hour time duration for macrophages treated with 400 nM NhhA; \*p < 0.05

> _Bar graph: Percent of apoptotic cells (y-axis, 0–30%) vs. Time in hours (0, 5, 10, 15, 20). Apoptosis rises
> time-dependently from ~2% (0 h) to ~20% (20 h); significant (\*) at 10, 15, and 20 h._

### Question 46 ✗ (missed)

What level of protein structure is disrupted by the point mutation in the C-terminus of NhhA?

- I. Primary
- II. Secondary
- III. Tertiary
- IV. Quaternary

- _Subtopic 1074 — General · Topic 19 — Protein Structures · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles ·
  Discipline 1: Biochemistry_

- A. III only
- B. I and II only
- **C. I and IV only ✓ (correct)**
- D. I, II, and III only _(your answer)_

**Solution:** Choice C is the correct answer. Because the primary structure is defined as the amino acid sequence, a
point mutation will always change primary structure as long as the mutation results in substitution of an amino acid
(Roman Numeral I). The only time a mutation would not alter the primary sequence is when the mutation resulted in a
related codon that still coded for the same amino acid. That is not possible in this case because the passage tells us
that the mutation reduced trimerization — so there must have been a change to the protein. This change must have impacted
the **quaternary structure** because the passage indicates that the point mutation disrupts the trimer formation of NhhA,
and trimerization is an example of quaternary structure. Answers A, B, and D are all incorrect because none of them
include quaternary structure (Roman Numeral IV). Also, it is possible that this point mutation does not alter the
secondary and tertiary structure of NhhA. The primary structure can be changed such that an amino acid is substituted but
the region still remains formed into a beta sheet or alpha helix, and the tertiary structure may still remain the same
(eliminating Roman Numerals II and III). That one amino acid could be the binding point for the trimer such that the
tertiary structures of the native protein and the mutant protein are indistinguishable, but trimer formation is
impossible in the mutant form.

### Question 47 ✓ (correct)

A researcher discovers an extremely virulent strain of _N. meningitidis_ that has four additional amino acids at the C-terminus of NhhA. These four amino acids most likely:

- _Subtopic 60 — 4° structure of proteins · Topic 19 — Protein Structures · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 1: Biochemistry_

- **A. stabilize the NhhA trimer. ✓ (correct)**
- B. alter the function of NhhA.
- C. localize NhhA to the nucleus of the macrophage.
- D. stabilize the NhhA monomer.

**Solution:** Answer choice A is the best answer. The passage mentions that mutations blocking trimerization of NhhA decrease the infectivity of the bacteria. A mutation that greatly increases infectivity, especially if that mutation is on the C-terminus, is therefore likely to stabilize the trimer of NhhA. Answer B is incorrect because nothing in the passage discusses the actual function of NhhA. Without any information on function there is no logical way to infer that a change in function would increase virulence rather than decrease it. Also, it is unlikely that a 4-amino acid addition at the tail end of a protein will alter its entire function. Answer C is incorrect because there is no reason to expect that localizing a bacterial protein to the nucleus of the macrophages responding to that bacterium will increase infectivity. According to the passage, _N. meningitidis_ is already causing apoptosis of macrophages, so nothing that might happen at the nucleus could be more damaging to the host than complete cell death. Answer D is incorrect because stabilizing the monomer would make the trimer less favorable. Based on passage information, anything that decreases trimerization will decrease the infectivity of the bacteria.

### Question 48 ✓ (correct)

Which experiment, together with the results presented in the passage, would most strongly support the conclusion that the increased rate of apoptosis shown in Figure 2 is dependent on NhhA?

- _Subtopic 352 — Oncogenes, apoptosis · Topic 128 — Biosignalling · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 2: Biology_

- A. Repeat the experiment using 800 nM NhhA.
- B. Double the time of the experiment to 40 hours.
- **C. Repeat the experiment, using 400 nM albumin in place of NhhA. ✓ (correct)**
- D. Repeat the experiment with an anti-apoptotic agent.

**Solution:** Answer choice C is the correct answer. To determine whether the cells are going through apoptosis because of the treatment with NhhA and not for some reason related to the cell culture, one could repeat the experiment without NhhA, adding the same amount (400 nM) of an inert control such as serum albumin. Albumin does not induce apoptosis, so this control would show that adding this amount of protein to the culture does not induce apoptosis on its own. Answer A is incorrect because doubling the amount of NhhA would increase the total amount of protein added. This would add another variable to the experiment. Answer B is incorrect because extending the experiment time would just extend the experiment, not show whether the apoptosis is due to NhhA. Answer D is incorrect because you would expect that adding an anti-apoptosis agent will decrease apoptosis no matter what the cause of that apoptosis, whether that apoptosis is caused by NhhA or some other source.
