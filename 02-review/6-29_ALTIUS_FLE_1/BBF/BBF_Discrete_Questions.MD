### Question 11

Which technique is preferred for purification of a protein with an {affinity tag}?

- *Subtopic 1103 — General · Topic 178 — Separations and Purifications · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 5: Organic Chemistry*

- A. {Size-exclusion chromatography}
- B. {Anion-exchange chromatography}
- C. {Cation-exchange chromatography}
- **D. {Metal-affinity chromatography} ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** Choice D is correct; metal-affinity chromatography is the only affinity column among the answers. The affinity tag added to the{ protein is most likely a **HIS-tag**}, which would bind Ni²⁺. Answer A would separate based on size, but this would not be effective unless another step was completed first. Answers B and C would separate based on charge as well. All could work, but are not likely to separate out all of the proteins without additional steps. Affinity chromatography could purify the protein in one step under the right conditions.

### Question 12

A diagram of the { ATP synthase | whats synthase? not just atp synthase, but like all synthases? hows it different from a kinase? whatsa kinase? } enzyme is shown. A toxic compound that blocks the {α subunits of ATP synthase} must enter which region of the mitochondria to function?

*(Diagram: H⁺ flows through the inner mitochondrial membrane; the α subunit sits below the membrane; ADP + Pᵢ → ATP.)*

- *Subtopic 253 — Mitochondria: Site of ATP production · Topic 46 — Membrane-Bound Organelles and Defining Characteristics of Eukaryotic Cells · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles · Discipline 2: Biology*

- A. {Cristae}
- **B. {Matrix} ✓ (correct)**
- C. {Intermembrane space}
- D. Outer mitochondrial membrane

**Solution:** Choice B is correct; the matrix is the innermost compartment of the mitochondria (the area below the membrane in the diagram). The proton channel {moiety} of ATP synthase spans the inner mitochondrial membrane, but the α and β subunits extend into the **matrix** compartment. Given this location, the compound would have to cross through the intermembrane space, making C incorrect. The cristae are the folds of the inner mitochondrial membrane, so the compound would also have to cross these membranes to function, making A incorrect. D is incorrect because the outer mitochondrial membrane is the lipid bilayer surrounding the entire organelle; if the compound were within this membrane it would be separated entirely from the matrix and ATP synthase.

### Question 28 ✗ (missed)

A proposed drug molecule is a structural analogue of the primary substrate of succinyl-CoA synthetase. This drug is
likely to have what impact on _K_~M~ and _V_~max~?

- *Subtopic 81 — Mixed Inhibition · Topic 22 — Control of Enzyme Activity · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles · Discipline 1: Biochemistry*

- **A. _K_~M~ will increase and _V_~max~ will remain the same ✓ (correct)**
- B. _K_~M~ will decrease and _V_~max~ will decrease *(your answer)*
- C. _K_~M~ will remain the same and _V_~max~ will decrease
- D. _K_~M~ will increase and _V_~max~ will decrease

**Solution:** Choice A is the correct answer. The scenario describes **competitive inhibition** because the drug analogue will compete with the substrate at the active site. Competitive inhibition causes an increase in the apparent _K_~M~ but no change in the _V_~max~. A primary characteristic of competitive inhibition is that it can be overcome at high substrate concentrations. Answer B is true for uncompetitive inhibition. Answer C is true for pure non-competitive inhibition. Answer D is not associated with any specific form of enzyme inhibition. Mixed inhibition could cause this result, but mixed inhibition can either increase or decrease _K_~M~, depending on whether the inhibitor has a higher affinity for E–S or for E.

### Question 29 ✗ (missed)

Which expression gives the probability that a mother and father will have four children, two girls and two boys, in any birth order?

- *Subtopic 1118 — General · Topic 34 — Meiosis and Other Factors Affecting Genetic Variability · CAR SIR 2: Reasoning Within the Text · Discipline 2: Biology*

- A. (½)⁴ *(your answer)*
- B. 4 (½)
- C. 2 (½)⁴
- **D. 6 (½)⁴ ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** Choice D is the correct answer. Each birth is an independent event. For each "birth event" the probability of having a boy is ½ and the probability of having a girl is also ½. This type of problem would be more simple had the question asked "What is the probability of having four children, all of them boys?" The probability of having four boys in a row is (1/2)⁴ or 1/16. However, the problem changes when the question states that the births can appear in any order. In this case, one must first predict all of the possible birth orders by which these parents could have two boys and two girls: **GGBB, GBGB, GBBG, BBGG, BGBG, BGGB** — six permutations. Next, calculate the possibility of each birth order. Because the probabilities of having a boy or a girl are both ½, the probability of the first order is ½ × ½ × ½ × ½ = (½)⁴ = 1/16. The math is identical for the other five birth orders. The final key is to recognize that six birth orders are six different events, ANY of which could satisfy the requirement of two boys + two girls, in any order. When multiple events are possible and any of those events satisfy the question, the probabilities of each event must be **ADDED**. Therefore, the chance of any of them happening is: 1/16 + 1/16 + 1/16 + 1/16 + 1/16 + 1/16 = 6(1/16), equivalent to Answer D: 6(1/2)⁴. This proof eliminates Answers A, B and C.

### Question 31 ✗ (missed)

Passive immunity could result from:

- I. receiving a vaccine.
- II. contracting a viral or bacterial infection.
- III. receiving an injection of antibodies.

- *Subtopic 1086 — General · Topic 141 — Immune System · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles · Discipline 2: Biology*

- **A. III only ✓ (correct)**
- B. I and II only
- C. II and III only
- D. I, II, and III *(your answer)*

**Solution:** Answer choice A is correct. Passive immunity is immunity generated when one receives antibodies from some outside source. The immune system of the person receiving the passive immunity is never directly activated. This can occur when a person is injected with antibodies developed against an antigen in another person or host, as in Statement III. A more common example is the antibodies passed from mother to child in mother's milk. Because Statement II is false, Answers B, C and D must be rejected.

## Terms & Notes

### Q11 — chromatography / protein purification

**Affinity (tag)** — An **affinity tag** is a small handle engineered onto a protein so it can be grabbed by a column that *specifically binds that handle*. The most common is a **His-tag** (a run of histidines), which binds immobilized **Ni²⁺**. "Affinity" = separation by a *specific binding partner*, not by a bulk property like size or charge.
*MCAT relevance:* the key pattern — give a protein a unique tag, then use the matching **affinity column** to pull it out in essentially one step.

**Size-exclusion chromatography (SEC)** — Separates by **size**. The column is packed with porous beads: **small** proteins enter the pores → long winding path → elute **last**; **large** proteins are excluded from the pores → flow straight through → elute **first**. (Counterintuitive: big = fast.) Also called gel-filtration.
*MCAT relevance:* "separate by molecular weight" → SEC; remember large elutes first.

**Anion-exchange chromatography** — Separates by **charge**; the resin is **positively** charged, so it binds **negatively-charged** proteins (anions stick). A protein is net-negative when pH is **above** its pI.
*MCAT relevance:* memory hook — the bead binds the *opposite* charge; anion-exchange binds anions because the resin is cationic.

**Cation-exchange chromatography** — The mirror image: the resin is **negatively** charged, so it binds **positively-charged** proteins (cations stick). A protein is net-positive when pH is **below** its pI. (Anion- + cation-exchange together = **ion-exchange chromatography**.)
*MCAT relevance:* pick anion vs. cation from the protein's net charge, which depends on pH relative to pI.

**Metal-affinity chromatography** *(the answer)* — A type of **affinity** chromatography using a fixed metal ion (commonly **Ni²⁺**) that specifically binds a **His-tag**. Only the tagged protein sticks; everything else washes off; elute the target with **imidazole**. Because it's recognition rather than a bulk property, it can purify a tagged protein in **one step**.
*MCAT relevance:* affinity tag → matching affinity column; this is why D beats the size/charge methods (A/B/C), which need extra steps and may not fully separate.

### Q12 — ATP synthase / mitochondria

**ATP synthase — and "synthase" vs "kinase" generally** —
↳ *Your note ("what's a synthase? all synthases? how's it different from a kinase? what's a kinase?"):* these are **enzyme-class names** based on the reaction type:
- **Synthase** — joins two molecules into one (a synthesis/condensation), classically **without** directly hydrolyzing ATP to drive it. (The closely-named **synthetase / ligase** does the same kind of joining but **does** spend ATP.)
- **Kinase** — transfers a **phosphate group**, almost always *from ATP* onto a substrate (phosphorylation). The reverse (removing a phosphate) is a **phosphatase**.
- **ATP synthase is a special, slightly misleading case:** it's named for its product — it builds **ATP from ADP + Pᵢ** — but it isn't powered by a normal condensation; it's driven by the **proton gradient** (protons flowing down their gradient through it). Mechanistically it's a proton-driven molecular motor (an ATPase run in reverse), not a typical "synthase."
*MCAT relevance:* know the enzyme-suffix shortcuts (kinase = adds phosphate from ATP; phosphatase = removes; dehydrogenase = redox/removes H; synthase vs. synthetase = build without vs. with ATP). They let you guess function from a name you've never seen.

**α subunits of ATP synthase** — ATP synthase has two parts: **F₀** (embedded in the inner membrane, forms the proton channel) and **F₁** (the knob sticking into the **matrix**, built from **α₃β₃** subunits where ATP is actually made). The catalytic sites sit mainly on the **β** subunits; the **α** subunits are structural/regulatory and, like β, project into the matrix — which is why a compound blocking them must reach the matrix.
*MCAT relevance:* F₀ = membrane proton channel; F₁ (α/β head) = matrix-facing catalytic knob. Protons flow F₀ → drives rotation → F₁ makes ATP.

**Cristae** — The **folds of the inner mitochondrial membrane**. The folding increases surface area to pack in more electron-transport-chain complexes and ATP synthase.
*MCAT relevance:* more cristae = more capacity for oxidative phosphorylation.

**Matrix** *(the answer)* — The **innermost** mitochondrial compartment, enclosed by the inner membrane. Holds the **citric acid (Krebs) cycle** enzymes, mitochondrial DNA, and the F₁ head of ATP synthase.
*MCAT relevance:* matrix = site of the TCA cycle and the catalytic end of ATP synthase; protons flow *into* the matrix to make ATP.

**Intermembrane space** — The compartment **between** the outer and inner membranes. The electron transport chain pumps **H⁺ into** here, making it the high-proton reservoir that powers ATP synthase.
*MCAT relevance:* high [H⁺] / low pH side; protons flow from here, through ATP synthase, back into the matrix (chemiosmosis).

**Moiety** — A general word for a **distinct part or portion of a molecule** (a functional segment). "The proton channel **moiety** of ATP synthase" = the part of the enzyme that forms the proton channel (i.e., F₀).
*MCAT relevance:* pure vocabulary — "moiety" = "a part of the molecule"; common in passages describing functional groups or subunits.

### Q28 — Enzyme kinetics & inhibition

**KM (Michaelis constant)** — The substrate concentration [S] at which reaction rate V = **½ Vmax**. Lower KM = higher affinity (enzyme reaches half-max at lower [S]). Formally: KM = (koff + kcat) / kon — ratio of E–S complex breakdown to formation. KM is NOT a true dissociation constant unless kcat << koff.
*MCAT relevance:* use KM as a proxy for affinity; drug that raises apparent KM is weakening the enzyme's ability to grab substrate.

**Vmax** — Maximum reaction rate when enzyme is fully saturated with substrate ([S] >> KM). Vmax = kcat × [E]total. kcat = **turnover number** = substrate molecules converted per enzyme per second. Vmax cannot be increased by adding more substrate once all active sites are occupied.
*MCAT relevance:* if Vmax is unchanged, more substrate can eventually restore normal rate; if Vmax is lowered, it cannot.

**Michaelis-Menten equation** — V = Vmax[S] / (KM + [S])
- At [S] = KM → V = Vmax/2
- At [S] >> KM (saturating) → V → Vmax (zero-order in [S])
- At [S] << KM → V ≈ (Vmax/KM)[S] (linear; first-order in [S])
*MCAT relevance:* plug in multiples of KM to estimate V as a fraction of Vmax (e.g., [S] = 9 KM → V = 9/10 Vmax).

**Lineweaver-Burke (double-reciprocal) plot** — Linearization of Michaelis-Menten:
1/V = (KM/Vmax)(1/[S]) + 1/Vmax
- **y-intercept = 1/Vmax** (read Vmax directly)
- **x-intercept = −1/KM** (the more negative, the smaller KM)
- **slope = KM/Vmax**
Used to identify inhibitor type: competitive shifts x-intercept right (KM↑), noncompetitive shifts y-intercept up (Vmax↓), uncompetitive produces a parallel line shifted up-left (both intercepts change, slope same).

**Competitive inhibition** — Inhibitor is a structural analogue of the substrate; competes directly at the active site. **KM↑, Vmax unchanged.** Flooding with substrate displaces the inhibitor → normal Vmax eventually reached. On L-B: same y-intercept (1/Vmax same), x-intercept shifts right, slope increases.
*MCAT hook:* "structural analogue" in the question stem = competitive. Q28 was exactly this (drug analogue of succinyl-CoA synthetase substrate).

**Noncompetitive (pure) inhibition** — Binds an **allosteric site** on free E or E–S with equal affinity; does NOT compete with substrate. **KM unchanged, Vmax↓.** Cannot be overcome by adding more substrate. On L-B: same x-intercept (−1/KM same), y-intercept rises, slope increases.
*MCAT hook:* KM stays the same because the enzyme still binds substrate normally — it just can't work at full capacity.

**Uncompetitive inhibition** — Binds **only the E–S complex** (not free enzyme). Trapping substrate in the complex raises apparent affinity → **KM↓**. Also **Vmax↓** (E–S can't complete the reaction). On L-B: **parallel lines** (slope unchanged), both y-intercept↑ and x-intercept shifts right.
*MCAT hook:* both KM and Vmax go down together; unique parallel-line signature on L-B.

**Mixed inhibition** — Binds both free E and E–S, but with different affinities. **Vmax always↓.** KM direction depends on preference:
- Prefers free E → KM↑ (more competitive-like)
- Prefers E–S → KM↓ (more uncompetitive-like)
On L-B: lines intersect left of y-axis. (Q28 answer D described this — "not associated with any specific form.")

**Summary table (memorize this):**

| Inhibitor type | KM | Vmax | Overcomeable by ↑[S]? | Binds |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Competitive | ↑ | same | ✓ yes | active site (E only) |
| Noncompetitive | same | ↓ | ✗ no | allosteric (E or E–S equally) |
| Uncompetitive | ↓ | ↓ | ✗ no | allosteric (E–S only) |
| Mixed | ↑ or ↓ | ↓ | ✗ no | allosteric (E and E–S, unequally) |

### Question 58 ✗ (missed)

What is the most probable impact of a point mutation that substitutes the terminal guanine of a 5' exon with an alternate nucleotide?

- _Subtopic 105 — Transfer RNA (tRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA) · Topic 27 — Transcription · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 2: Biology_

- A. The mRNA will be prematurely terminated.
- B. The protein will be nonfunctional due to an amino acid change. _(your answer)_
- **C. The intron will not be spliced from the mRNA transcript. ✓ (correct)**
- D. There will be no effect, because this is a silent mutation.

**Solution:** Answer choice C is the correct answer. A guanine at the 5' position is required for RNA splicing. If a different nucleotide were present, the process would not function and the intron would not be removed. This makes choice C the best answer. This will not create a stop codon, making choice A incorrect. Answer B may be tempting because failure of the splicing mechanism may well result in a non-functional protein. However, given both answers B and C, answer C is a more complete and accurate description that emphasizes the primary importance of guanine in the splicing mechanism. This is particularly important given that the importance of a guanine substitution was specifically referenced in the stem. Examinees should expect AAMC questions to feature distractors that may be true, or could be true, but are not as complete, or as correct, as the best answer. Answer D must be incorrect because, as already described, failure of exon splicing will have multiple deleterious effects.
