## Terms & Notes

### 2,6,10,14-tetramethylpentadecane вЂ” IUPAC nomenclature for branched alkanes

Your question: *what does the numbering mean?*

Break the name into parts:

| Name chunk | What it means |
|---|---|
| **pentadecane** | The parent chain has **15 carbons** (penta = 5, deca = 10; pentadeca = 15). It's an alkane (all single bonds), so suffix "-ane." |
| **tetramethyl** | **Four methyl groups** (CHв‚ѓ) are branching off the main chain (tetra = 4). |
| **2, 6, 10, 14** | The carbon **positions** on the 15-carbon chain where each methyl branch attaches. |

**How the numbering works:** Number the main chain from whichever end gives the substituents the *lowest* set of numbers. Here 2,6,10,14 is lower than the alternative from the other end (2,6,10,14 vs. 2,6,10,14 вЂ” this molecule is symmetric, so either direction gives the same numbers). The rule: always minimize the locant set.

*MCAT relevance:* You won't need to name complex structures from scratch, but you should be able to read an IUPAC name and know (a) the parent-chain length from the root word, (b) the substituents and their count from the prefix, and (c) that numbers = positions on the chain.

### macrophages

Large, long-lived phagocytic cells of the **innate immune system**. They arise from circulating **monocytes** (a myeloid blood cell) that exit the bloodstream and mature in tissues. Core functions:

- **Phagocytosis** вЂ” engulf and destroy pathogens, dead cells, and debris.
- **Antigen presentation** вЂ” display peptide fragments on MHC II to activate helper T cells (bridging innate в†’ adaptive immunity).
- **Cytokine secretion** вЂ” release signaling molecules (e.g., TNF, IL-1) to amplify inflammation and recruit more immune cells.

In this passage: pristane triggers chronic inflammation в†’ macrophages are recruited to granulomas в†’ RORОіtвЃ»/вЃ» mice recruit far fewer macrophages, implicating RORОіt in that recruitment process.

*MCAT relevance:* macrophages = the innate system's "cleanup crew" AND a key link to adaptive immunity via antigen presentation. Monocyte (blood) в†’ macrophage (tissue) is a tested differentiation.

### mesentery

A double fold of **peritoneum** (the membrane lining the abdominal cavity) that attaches the small intestine to the posterior abdominal wall. Think of it as a "fan-shaped sheet" suspending the intestines so they don't flop around. It carries:
- blood vessels (branches of the superior mesenteric artery/vein)
- lymphatic vessels
- nerves

In this passage: pristane is injected intraperitoneally в†’ inflammatory granulomas form in the mesentery (MG = mesenteric granuloma) and along its edges near the intestine (SG = serosal granuloma). The mesentery is just the anatomical site of inflammation here.

*MCAT relevance:* mostly anatomy context for passages вЂ” you need to know it's an abdominal membrane anchoring the gut, not an organ with a special biochemical role.

### progenitor

A **progenitor cell** is an early, partially committed precursor that can divide and differentiate into a limited set of mature cell types. Key distinction:

- **Stem cell** вЂ” self-renewing + pluripotent; can become many lineages and make more stem cells.
- **Progenitor** вЂ” limited self-renewal; already committed to a lineage; cannot revert to a less-committed state.

Here: **hematopoietic progenitor** = the common ancestor in bone marrow from which all blood cells descend. The lineage splits into:
- **Common myeloid progenitor (CMP)** в†’ monocytes, macrophages, neutrophils, basophils, eosinophils, erythrocytes, dendritic cells, megakaryocytes/platelets.
- **Common lymphoid progenitor (CLP)** в†’ T cells, B cells, NK cells.

*MCAT relevance:* know which cells are myeloid vs. lymphoid. Myeloid = most innate immune cells + RBCs + platelets. Lymphoid = adaptive immune cells (T, B) + NK cells.
