# [CLUE] Immune System (innate vs. adaptive, passive vs. active, primary vs. secondary)
> Cover everything below and lecture it from memory.
> (From Altius FLE 1 Q19, Q30, Q31 — Bio weakness, 3 misses. Core confusion: "non-specific adaptive" does not exist; passive ≠ vaccination.)

- **Two arms of immunity**
  - **Innate (non-specific)**
    - first responders — no memory, no antigen-specific recognition
    - cells: **macrophages**, neutrophils, dendritic cells, NK cells, mast cells
    - mechanisms: **inflammation**, phagocytosis, fever, complement
    - fast (minutes–hours)
    - Note: non-specific = same response regardless of pathogen. Inflammation IS innate.
  - **Adaptive (specific)**
    - antigen-specific, slower to mount but has memory
    - two sub-types (both are specific — there is NO "non-specific adaptive"):
      - **Cell-mediated** — T cells; kills infected cells directly
      - **Humoral** — B cells → plasma cells → **antibodies**
    - Note: "non-specific adaptive immunity" does not exist — this was the trap in Q19. Innate = non-specific. Adaptive = always specific.

- **How an antigen becomes adaptive — the bridge**
  - macrophages phagocytose pathogen → display antigen on **MHC II** → activate **helper T cells**
  - helper T cells activate B cells (humoral) and cytotoxic T cells (cell-mediated)
  - Note: macrophages are innate, but antigen presentation is how innate hands off to adaptive.

- **Primary vs. secondary immune response**
  - **Primary** — first exposure to antigen
    - slow ramp-up (~1–2 weeks), lower peak antibody titer
    - generates **memory B cells** and **memory T cells** (the lasting record)
  - **Secondary** — re-exposure to same antigen
    - **faster and more efficient** — memory cells respond immediately
    - higher antibody titer, longer-lasting
    - Note: Q30 trap: BOTH responses stimulate B cells to make antibodies. Primary response does NOT stimulate memory T cells while secondary stimulates memory B cells — both generate both. The difference is speed/magnitude.

- **Active vs. passive immunity**
  - **Active** — your own immune system makes the antibodies
    - **active natural** = catching an infection (your body fights it, makes memory)
    - **active artificial** = vaccination (antigen exposure triggers your immune response)
    - lasting; builds immunological memory
  - **Passive** — you RECEIVE antibodies made by someone/something else
    - your immune system is NOT activated
    - examples: maternal antibodies via breast milk, injection of antibody serum (antivenom, immunoglobulin shot)
    - short-lived (no memory cells formed)
    - Note: Q31 trap: vaccination (I) = active, not passive — it activates YOUR immune system. Contracting infection (II) = also active. Only receiving ready-made antibodies (III) = passive.
    - Note: mnemonic — **passive** = you just sit there and receive; **active** = your body actively fights.
