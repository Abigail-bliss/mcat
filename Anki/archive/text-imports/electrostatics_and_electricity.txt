<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric charge</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Charge</b><br><br>A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electric field.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>q or Q</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Coulombs (C)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric force</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Force</b><br><br>The attractive or repulsive force between two charged objects. <div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is an <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric field</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Field</b><br><br>A region around a charged object where another charge experiences a force. Direction is defined as the direction a <i>positive</i> test charge would move.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>E</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Newtons per Coulomb (N/C) = Volts per meter (V/m)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric potential (voltage)</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Potential (Voltage)</b><br><br>The electric potential energy per unit charge at a point in space.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>V</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Volts (V = J/C)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric potential energy</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Potential Energy</b><br><br>The energy stored in a system of charges due to their positions relative to each other.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>U</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Joules (J = kg·m²/s²)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>capacitance</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Capacitance</b><br><br>A measure of a capacitor's ability to store charge per unit voltage.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>C</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Farads (F = C/V)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric current</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Current</b><br><br>The rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>I</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Amperes (A = C/s)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>resistance</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Resistance</b><br><br>A material's opposition to the flow of electric current.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>R</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Ohms (Ω = V/A)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electrical power</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electrical Power</b><br><br>The rate at which electrical energy is transferred or dissipated in a circuit.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>P</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Watts (W = J/s)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>resistivity</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Resistivity</b><br><br>An intrinsic property of a material describing how strongly it resists current flow, independent of its shape or size.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>ρ (rho)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Ohm·meters (Ω·m)</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Higher ρ = worse conductor</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Definition</b><br><br>What is <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>voltage</span> in the context of a circuit?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Voltage (in a circuit)</b><br><br>The potential difference between two points in a circuit — the "push" that drives current from high to low potential.<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Symbol</b></td><td>V</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Unit</b></td><td>Volts (V = J/C)</td></tr></table></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)</b><br><br>What does Kirchhoff's Current Law state?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)</b><br><br>The sum of all currents entering a junction equals the sum of all currents leaving it.<br><br><span style='font-size: 15px; color: #555;'>"What goes in must come out"</span><br><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Based on conservation of charge</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)</b><br><br>What does Kirchhoff's Voltage Law state?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)</b><br><br>The sum of all voltage changes around any closed loop in a circuit equals zero.<br><br><span style='font-size: 15px; color: #555;'>Voltage gained = Voltage dropped</span><br><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Based on conservation of energy</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Coulomb's Law</b><br><br>What is the equation for the <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric force</span> between two point charges?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Coulomb's Law</b><br><br>\[F = k\dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\]<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>F</b></td><td>electric force — Newtons (N = kg·m/s²)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>k</b></td><td>8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>q₁, q₂</b></td><td>point charges — Coulombs (C)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>r</b></td><td>distance between charges — meters (m)</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Force falls off as 1/r²</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Field — Point Charge</b><br><br>What is the equation for the <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric field</span> produced by a point charge?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Field — Point Charge</b><br><br>\[E = k\dfrac{q}{r^2}\]<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>E</b></td><td>electric field — N/C or V/m</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>k</b></td><td>8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>q</b></td><td>source charge — Coulombs (C)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>r</b></td><td>distance from charge — meters (m)</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Field falls off as 1/r²</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Force in a Field</b><br><br>What is the <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>force on a charge</span> placed inside an electric field?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Force in a Field</b><br><br>\[F = qE\]<br><br><span style='font-size: 14px; color: #555;'>Expanded (substituting E = kq/r²):</span><br>\[F = \dfrac{kq\, q_{source}}{r^2}\]<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>F</b></td><td>electric force — Newtons (N)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>q</b></td><td>charge in the field — Coulombs (C)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>E</b></td><td>electric field — N/C</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>This is Coulomb's Law approached from the field perspective</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Potential — Point Charge</b><br><br>What is the equation for the <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric potential (voltage)</span> at a distance r from a point charge?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Potential — Point Charge</b><br><br>\[V = k\dfrac{q}{r}\]<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>V</b></td><td>electric potential — Volts (V = J/C)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>k</b></td><td>8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>q</b></td><td>source charge — Coulombs (C)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>r</b></td><td>distance from charge — meters (m)</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Potential falls off as 1/r — not 1/r²</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Potential Energy</b><br><br>What is the equation for the <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric potential energy</span> between two point charges?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Potential Energy</b><br><br>\[U = k\dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r}\]<br><br><span style='font-size: 14px; color: #555;'>Also expressible as:</span><br>\[U = qV\]<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>U</b></td><td>potential energy — Joules (J)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>q₁, q₂</b></td><td>point charges — Coulombs (C)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>r</b></td><td>distance between charges — meters (m)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>V</b></td><td>electric potential at that point — Volts (V)</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>Falls off as 1/r — not 1/r²</span></div>
<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Field &amp; Potential Relationship</b><br><br>How is the <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric field</span> related to <span style='color: #4a90d9;'>electric potential</span>?</div>	<div style='font-family: Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 18px; text-align: center; padding: 20px;'><b>Electric Field &amp; Potential Relationship</b><br><br>\[E = -\dfrac{\Delta V}{\Delta r}\]<br><br><table style='margin: auto; text-align: left; font-size: 15px;'><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>E</b></td><td>electric field — N/C or V/m</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>ΔV</b></td><td>change in potential — Volts (V)</td></tr><tr><td style='padding: 4px 10px;'><b>Δr</b></td><td>change in distance — meters (m)</td></tr></table><br><span style='font-size: 13px; color: #888;'>E points from high V to low V (hence the negative sign)</span></div>
