# Biological and Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems (BB) Section

## Passage 3 (Questions 15–18)

Amazonian religious healers use native plants as medicinal treatments for bacterial infections. To investigate the
biological activity of these plant compounds, scientists {lyophilized} organs from several commonly used plants, generated
plant extracts of known concentrations, and used an agar diffusion test to evaluate each extract for antimicrobial
activity.

Microorganisms were first grown on agar plates. Each agar plate contained holes filled with either plant extract, or
ciprofloxacin, a proven synthetic antibiotic. All plant extracts were prepared by dissolving lyophilized powders in
water at a concentration of 2.28 mg/mL. 30 µL of ciprofloxacin (16.6 µg/mL) was inserted into a hole in the center of
each agar plate, and 30 µL of each plant extract was inserted into surrounding holes. The inhibition zones (IZ) around
each hole were measured and reported (Table 1).

**Table 1** — IZ measurements for microorganisms treated with Amazonian plant extracts

| Category          | Botanical extract or Control | Microorganism Treated               | IZ Size (mean ± SD, mm) |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| **Gram-Positive** | _Symphonia globulifera_      | _Staphylococcus aureus_             | 3.66 ± 0.76             |
|                   | _Vatairea guianensis_        | _Staphylococcus aureus_             | 8.0 ± 0.5               |
|                   | _Hymenelobium petraeum_      | _Staphylococcus aureus_             | 3.66 ± 0.57             |
|                   | Ciprofloxacin                | _Staphylococcus aureus_             | 9 ± 0                   |
|                   | _Hymenelobium petraeum_      | _Enterococcus faecalis_             | 5 ± 0.1                 |
|                   | Ciprofloxacin                | _Enterococcus faecalis_             | 11 ± 0                  |
| **Gram-Negative** | _Hymenelobium petraeum_      | _Salmonella enterica_ serovar Typhi | 7 ± 0.26                |
|                   | Ciprofloxacin                | _Salmonella enterica_ serovar Typhi | 10 ± 0                  |
|                   | _Pentaclethra macroloba_     | _Klebsiella ozanae_                 | 3.96 ± 0.15             |
|                   | _Ptychopetalum olacoides_    | _Klebsiella ozanae_                 | 9.25 ± 0.35             |
|                   | Ciprofloxacin                | _Klebsiella ozanae_                 | 8 ± 1.4                 |
|                   | _Pentaclethra macroloba_     | _Acinetobacter baumannii_           | 5.76 ± 0.25             |
|                   | _Ptychopetalum olacoides_    | _Acinetobacter baumannii_           | 4.9 ± 0.14              |
|                   | Ciprofloxacin                | _Acinetobacter baumannii_           | 11 ± 0                  |
| **Yeast**         | _Hymenelobium petraeum_      | _Candida albicans_                  | 11.9 ± 0.1              |
|                   | Ciprofloxacin                | _Candida albicans_                  | 13.63 ± 0.32            |

Investigators assigned each plant extract an effectiveness rating, _Ef_, calculated using Equation 1, where _Pd_ is the
diameter in mm of the IZ around the plant extract, and _Cd_ is the diameter in mm of the IZ around ciprofloxacin.

**Equation 1:** _Ef_ = (_Pd_ / _Cd_) × 100

Bezerra, Paulo OM Junior and Luís IB Kanzaki. Antimicrobial activity of amazonian medicinal plants. SpringerPlus 2013,
2:371. doi:10.1186/2193-1801-2-371._

## Questions

### Question 15

_Enterococcus faecium_ is a { Gram-positive bacteria | whats a gram positive/gram negative bacteria? } responsible for
many hospital-based infections. Which plant extract is likely to be most effective against an _E. faecium_ infection?

- _Subtopic 1113 — General · Topic 123 — Growth and Physiology of Prokaryotic Cells · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and
  Problem Solving · Discipline 2: Biology_

- A. _Hymenelobium petraeum_
- B. _Ptychopetalum olacoides_
- C. _Enterococcus faecalis_
- **D. _Vatairea guianensis_ ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** Choice D is correct. The question hinges on recognizing that although _E. faecium_ isn't in Table 1, it is
**Gram-positive**, so extracts shown effective against Gram-positive strains are the best bet. Only two answer choices
(_Hymenelobium petraeum_ and _Vatairea guianensis_) are extracts tested on Gram-positive bacteria. B is wrong — there's
no data on _Ptychopetalum olacoides_ against Gram-positive bacteria. C is wrong — _Enterococcus faecalis_ is a
microorganism in Table 1, not a botanical extract (a trap if you confuse _E. faecium_ with _E. faecalis_). Of the two
valid extracts, _Vatairea guianensis_ produced the **largest IZ** against Gram-positive bacteria (8.0 mm vs. 3.66 mm),
so D beats A.

### Question 16

Culturing and identifying an infectious bacterial strain requires several days. If a severe case requires treatment
before identification of the microorganism is possible, which plant extract from Table 1 should be administered?

- _Subtopic 1113 — General · Topic 123 — Growth and Physiology of Prokaryotic Cells · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and
  Problem Solving · Discipline 2: Biology_

- **A. _Hymenelobium petraeum_ ✓ (correct)**
- B. _Ptychopetalum olacoides_
- C. _Pentaclethra macroloba_
- D. _Symphonia globulifera_

**Solution:** Choice A is correct. _H. petraeum_ is the only extract shown to be effective against **all** classes of
microorganism in Table 1 — Gram-positive bacteria, Gram-negative bacteria, and yeast. **Broad-spectrum** protection is
what's needed when the infectious agent's identity is unknown. B, C, and D have known efficacy only against
Gram-negative strains (or a narrower range), so they aren't the best choice.

### Question 17

Antibiotics that harm prokaryotic cells, but not eukaryotic cells, most likely target which cellular organelle?

- _Subtopic 1119 — General · Topic 122 — Classification and Structure of Prokaryotic Cells · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning
  and Problem Solving · Discipline 2: Biology_

- A. Nucleus
- B. Endoplasmic reticulum
- C. Plasma membrane
- **D. Cell wall ✓ (correct)**

**Solution:** Choice D is correct. Bacteria are prokaryotic and therefore lack a nucleus and other membrane-bound
organelles found in eukaryotic cells, ruling out A and B. Bacteria do have **peptidoglycan cell walls**, and
peptidoglycan is unique to the prokaryotic domain — so many antibiotics interfere with peptidoglycan/bacterial cell-wall
synthesis. C (plasma membrane) is not the best choice because **all** cells have a plasma membrane, so targeting it
wouldn't spare eukaryotes.

### Question 18

Researchers used extracts made from total plant organ biomass rather than the purified active ingredient. If the active
ingredient was 1.0% of the total plant biomass, the _Ef_ values reported by the researchers in this study:

- _Subtopic 1113 — General · Topic 123 — Growth and Physiology of Prokaryotic Cells · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design
  and Execution of Research · Discipline 2: Biology_

- **A. overestimated the effectiveness of the plant compounds. ✓ (correct)**
- B. underestimated the effectiveness of the plant compounds.
- C. overestimated the effectiveness of ciprofloxacin.
- D. underestimated the virulence of the microorganisms.

**Solution:** Choice A is correct. The key is recognizing that **mg/mL is 1000-fold more concentrated than µg/mL**. The
plant extract is 2.28 **mg**/mL = 2280 µg/mL; ciprofloxacin is 16.6 **µg**/mL. Per the stem, only 1.0% of the extract is
active ingredient → 22.8 µg/mL active ingredient. So when equal 30 µL volumes were added, the plates received **22.8
µg/mL active plant ingredient vs. only 16.6 µg/mL ciprofloxacin** — _more_ active plant compound than ciprofloxacin.
Thus the researchers **overestimated** the plant extracts' effectiveness (they used more active ingredient than the
comparison drug). B is wrong (they overestimated, not underestimated). C is wrong — _Ef_ reports plant effectiveness
_relative to_ ciprofloxacin (Equation 1), not ciprofloxacin's own effectiveness; if anything they could have
_underestimated_ ciprofloxacin's potency. This is a classic "difficulty through precision, not obscure content" question
— it tests whether you noticed the **unit mismatch (mg vs. µg)**.
