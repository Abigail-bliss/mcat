## Questions

### Question 7

Researchers included the sense-Agrin mRNA trial in Table 1 to demonstrate that:

- *Subtopic 98 — Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → protein · Topic 26 — Genetic Code · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 2: Biology*

- A. both modified oligonucleotides successfully bound their targets.
- B. the sense-Agrin strand is complementary to, and will bind, the anti-sense-Agrin strand.
- **C. the effects of anti-sense-Agrin mRNA could not be replicated by a non-binding oligonucleotide. ✓ (correct)**
- D. sense-Agrin mRNA strand was not translated.

**Solution:** Choice C is correct. The scientists are testing whether the anti-sense strand "turns off" the mRNA for Agrin. That's hard to prove, because anything with general effects on protein synthesis or metabolism could also reduce Agrin expression. The sense strand is an excellent **negative control**: chemically similar to the antisense strand but without its specific binding interaction with Agrin mRNA. A is wrong — including the sense-Agrin strand provides no proof that binding occurred. B is wrong — although the strands are complementary, they aren't included together in any treatment. D is wrong — nothing in Table 1 or the design relates to translation of the Agrin mRNA.

### Question 8

Which experimental result from the study of transgenic rats pre-disposed to AD would most strongly support the theory that a reduction in the number of dendritic spines causes AD?

- *Subtopic 1085 — General · Topic 132 — Nervous System: Structure and Function · SIR 3: Reasoning About the Design and Execution of Research · Discipline 2: Biology*

- A. The number of dendritic spines on cortical neurons is inversely correlated with the severity of AD symptoms.
- **B. Increasing the dosage of a drug that promotes dendritic spine formation is associated with a significant decrease in AD symptoms. ✓ (correct)**
- C. Symptom-free rats with a reduced number of dendritic spines on their cortical neurons eventually develop AD.
- D. A drug that inhibits dendritic spine formation on cortical neurons is associated with a significant increase in AD symptoms.

**Solution:** Choice B is correct. All answers offer some support that dendrite morphology is part of AD, but only B and D suggest (without proving) causality. A and C are strictly correlations and provide no evidence of causation. B is stronger than D because it implies a **dose-response effect**: not only does increasing dendritic spines decrease AD symptoms, but *more* drug (and therefore more spines) yields *even fewer* symptoms. A dosage effect is stronger evidence of causation than a simple association between treatment and outcome.

### Question 9

During embryological development, Agrin released from a somatic motor neuron causes acetylcholine receptors throughout the sarcolemma to migrate to the motor end plate. With which cellular structure is Agrin most likely to interact during NMJ formation?

- *Subtopic 461 — Nervous control: motor neurons · Topic 145 — Muscle System · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles · Discipline 2: Biology*

- **A. Myocyte plasma membrane ✓ (correct)**
- B. Nerve cell plasma membrane
- C. Myocyte cytoplasm
- D. Nerve cell cytoplasm

**Solution:** Choice A is correct. The acetylcholine receptors are located in the sarcolemma, the cell membrane of the muscle cell. It is therefore reasonable to assume that inducing the receptors to cluster at the motor end plate requires that Agrin interact with those receptors directly, or with the membrane in which they are located. B and D can be eliminated because Agrin is released from the axon terminal, and the sarcolemma is not found in axons. C can be eliminated because Agrin is a protein unlikely to travel to the muscle cell cytoplasm, given that neuromuscular junctions are found on the cell surface.

### Question 10

The oligonucleotide bound to mature Agrin mRNA at the end of the experiment is:

- *Subtopic 1116 — General · Topic 27 — Transcription · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 2: Biology*

- A. sense-Agrin mRNA, because it is complementary to the mature mRNA strand.
- B. sense-Agrin mRNA, because it is complementary to the coding strand.
- **C. anti-sense-Agrin mRNA, because it is complementary to the mature mRNA strand. ✓ (correct)**
- D. anti-sense-Agrin mRNA, because it is complementary to the DNA template strand.

**Solution:** Choice C is correct. A and B can be eliminated because it is the anti-sense-Agrin mRNA that binds the mature Agrin mRNA to prevent its translation, not the sense-Agrin mRNA. This is supported by Table 1, where the anti-sense-Agrin mRNA trial shows reduced dendrite length and spine number, but the sense-Agrin mRNA trial does not. D is false because the anti-sense-Agrin strand is **not** complementary to the template strand — it is complementary to the **coding strand**. With the exception of uracil substituting for thymine (and any post-transcriptional modifications), the anti-sense mRNA strand is identical to the template strand, since both are binding complements to the mRNA transcript.

## Terms & Notes

**Dendritic spines** — Small membranous protrusions sticking out from a neuron's dendrite; each is the *postsynaptic* receiving site for (usually) one excitatory synapse. Their number and shape change with activity, which is the physical basis of synaptic strength.
*MCAT relevance:* this is the structural unit behind **neuroplasticity / learning & memory** (long-term potentiation). More/larger spines ≈ stronger connectivity — exactly what the passage is measuring as Agrin is suppressed.

**36 kb gene** — `kb` = **kilobase** = 1,000 nucleotide bases (base pairs for double-stranded DNA). So a 36 kb gene is 36,000 bp long.
↳ *Your note ("what does kb mean?"):* it's a length unit for nucleic acids. Don't confuse it with **kDa** (kilodalton), which measures *protein mass*, not length.
*MCAT relevance:* you just need the unit; gene/sequence sizes are given in kb, protein sizes in kDa or # of amino acids.

**Neuromuscular junction (NMJ)** — The chemical synapse between a **motor neuron** and a **skeletal muscle fiber**. The neuron releases acetylcholine (ACh) → binds nicotinic ACh receptors on the muscle → depolarization → contraction.
*MCAT relevance:* the textbook example of a synapse; know ACh, acetylcholinesterase, nicotinic receptors, and that Agrin organizes ACh receptors here (why AGRN knockout disrupts NMJ development).

**Mouse knock-out studies** — Experiments where one specific gene is deliberately inactivated ("knocked out") in a mouse, then you observe what goes wrong. The resulting defect reveals the gene's normal job.
*MCAT relevance:* loss-of-function logic — if knocking out *AGRN* breaks NMJ development, Agrin must normally be *required* for it. This "remove it and see what fails" reasoning shows up constantly in experimental passages.

**Protein domains (LNS, KSPI, EGF)** —
↳ *Your note ("what's a domain? what's an inhibitor domain? do I need to memorize each?"):* **No, you don't need to memorize these specific names** — that's exactly the kind of detail the passage will re-tell you if a question needs it. What to actually know:
- A **domain** = a distinct, independently-folding *module* of a protein that does its own job. One protein can be built from several different domains (and repeat them), like Lego pieces — that's why Agrin is called "multidomain."
- An **inhibitor domain** = a module whose job is to *block* an enzyme. Here the **Kazal-type serine protease inhibitor (KSPI)** domain inhibits **serine proteases** (enzymes that cut other proteins).
*MCAT relevance:* the testable concept is **modular protein structure** (tertiary structure / functional units) and the idea of protease inhibition — not the proper nouns.

**Morphology** — The *form and structure* of something — here, the shape of the dendrites (their length, spine count, spine length). Contrast with *function*.
*MCAT relevance:* plain vocabulary; "morphology" = "what it looks like / structure," which is what Table 1 quantifies.

**Oligonucleotides** — Short, usually single-stranded strings of nucleotides (DNA or RNA), often just a handful up to ~50 bases, synthesized to base-pair with a chosen complementary target.
*MCAT relevance:* this is the tool behind **antisense knockdown** — design an oligo complementary to an mRNA so it binds and blocks translation (exactly the experiment here).

**Sense strand vs. anti-sense strand** —
↳ *Your note ("what are these?"):* the two strands of a gene's DNA:
- **Sense (coding) strand** — has the *same* sequence as the mRNA (with T instead of U).
- **Anti-sense (template) strand** — *complementary* to the mRNA; it's the strand RNA polymerase actually reads to build the mRNA.
- An oligo **complementary to the sense strand** is therefore complementary to the **mRNA** → it base-pairs with the mRNA and blocks its translation. That's the one expected to suppress Agrin (matching the big drops in the "Anti-sense Agrin mRNA" row of Table 1).
*MCAT relevance:* know coding vs. template strand and transcription direction; the trick is that "binds the mRNA" requires a sequence complementary to the mRNA (i.e., to the sense strand).
