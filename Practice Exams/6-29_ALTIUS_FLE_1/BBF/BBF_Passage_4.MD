# Biological and Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems (BB) Section

## Passage 4 (Questions 19–22)

_Reactive gliosis_, the proliferation and {hypertrophy} of neuroglial cells, is caused by brain trauma, such as acute
ischemia. Microglia growth and proliferation is initiated by signaling proteins secreted by injured neurons. Reversible
injury to neurons signals microglia to produce trophic factors, which promote neuron survival. In cases of irreversible
injury, microglia release neurotoxic factors that promote degeneration and apoptosis.

Researchers explored the effects of overstimulated microglial activity by culturing healthy brain explants from
embryonic rats. The neurons in the sample tissue {extended axons vigorously} during the first 24 to 48 hours of culture.
When co-cultured with {ischemic} explants from adult brain tissue, the neurons degenerated within 24 hours. The embryonic
explants also degenerated when incubated in the medium that previously harbored the ischemic brain cells, leading
scientists to speculate that the ischemic neurons released neurotoxic factors into the medium. These toxins were
identified and used to create a representative "conditioned" medium.

Two histone proteins, linker histone H1 and core histone H2B, were included in the conditioned medium. Purified H1 and
H2B were added to cultured embryonic brain explants. Cell culture growth patterns were graphed in Figure 1, using
relative fluorescence units (RLU), a measure of apoptotic proteases released during cell death.

**Figure 1** — Effect of exogenous histones H1 (A) and H2B (B) on the growth of cultured cortical neurons from embryonic
mice

> _**Panel A** — RLU (y-axis, 0–12k) vs. Histone H1 in nM (x-axis, 0–200). RLU stays low (~1–2k) up to ~50 nM, rises to
> ~5k at 100 nM, and reaches ~11k at 200 nM. → Apoptotic protease release **increases** with H1 dose._
> _**Panel B** — RLU vs. Histone H2B in nM (0–200). RLU remains low (~1–2k) across **all** concentrations. → H2B has **no**
> apoptotic effect on the neurons._

Researchers next examined the effect of Histone H1 on microglial cells. When microglial cells are grown in serum-free
culture at low concentration, apoptosis is observed within 24 hours. Microglia were grown under these same conditions,
but with increasing doses of H1, as seen in Figure 2.

**Figure 2** — Number of viable microglial cells in mediums of increasing H1 concentration

> _Bar chart, Cell Count (y-axis, 0–400) vs. Histone H1 in nM (0, 10, 50, 200). Counts rise from ~180 (0 nM) → ~270
> (10 nM) → ~320 (50 nM) → ~290 (200 nM). → Increasing H1 **increases** microglial survival/viability._

Researchers proposed a positive feedback mechanism to explain the dual effects of H1 on neuron degeneration and reactive
gliosis. Histone H1 released from dying neurons causes death of neighboring neurons and simultaneously promotes the
pro-inflammatory response in microglia, exacerbating neurodegeneration.

- B. humoral adaptive immunity.
- **C. non-specific innate immunity. ✓ (correct)**
- D. non-specific adaptive immunity. _(your answer)_

**Solution:** Choice C is correct. There are two types of immunity in animals, innate immunity and adaptive immunity.
There are also two kinds of adaptive immunity: cell-mediated and humoral immunity. In the final paragraph, the passage
indicates researchers concluded that microglial cells enhanced the inflammatory response. Because inflammation is
non-specific and part of an innate immune response, choice C is correct. Answers A and B can be eliminated because they
refer to adaptive immunity. Choice D refers to non-specific adaptive immunity, which **does not exist** — innate
immunity is described as non-specific, while adaptive immunity is always pathogen-specific.

**Solution:** Choice D is correct. According to the data in the graph, the rate of movement of microglial cells into the

### Question 21 ✗ (missed)

The histones released by ischemic neurons were primarily found in which cellular location prior to the ischemic trauma?

- _Subtopic 337 — Cell body: site of nucleus, organelles · Topic 133 — Nerve Cell · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and
  Problem Solving · Discipline 2: Biology_

- A. Dendrites
- **B. Soma ✓ (correct)**
- C. Synaptic end plate _(your answer)_
- D. Axon

**Solution:** Choice B is correct. This question requires recognizing the function of a histone and combining that
knowledge with an understanding of the structure of a neuron. Histones are the proteins around which DNA is wound in its
condensed conformation. In an intact cell (including neurons), histones will be primarily located in the nucleus,
associated with the DNA they stabilize. In a neuron, the nucleus is found in the soma, or cell body, of the neuron,
making B the only plausible answer. Because DNA is not present in the axon, synaptic end plate (distal part of the axon
at the synapse), or the dendrites (projections off of the soma), histones will not be found in these locations to any
significant degree, eliminating Answers A, C and D.

## Terms & Notes

**Hypertrophy** — An increase in cell/tissue/organ size due to **enlargement of existing cells** (not more cells). Contrast with **hyperplasia** = increase in cell *number*. Here, "reactive gliosis" = both the **proliferation** (hyperplasia) *and* **hypertrophy** of neuroglia.
*MCAT relevance:* know the pair — hypertrophy = bigger cells, hyperplasia = more cells (e.g., muscle growth from exercise = hypertrophy).

**Extended axons vigorously** — The embryonic neurons were actively growing — sending out **axonal processes** (axon outgrowth via growth cones). It's a marker of **healthy, viable, growing neurons**, set up as the baseline before they later *degenerated* when exposed to ischemic factors.
*MCAT relevance:* vigorous axon extension = healthy/developing neuron; the contrast (extension → degeneration) is the experiment's readout.

**Ischemic / ischemia** — **Inadequate blood supply** to a tissue → deprivation of **oxygen and glucose** → cell injury and death. "Acute ischemia" is the brain trauma driving reactive gliosis here.
*MCAT relevance:* ischemia → hypoxia → cell damage; the classic example is **stroke**. The ischemic adult cells are what release the neurotoxic factors (histones).

**"Neurotoxic factors" = the histones (the Q22 connection)** —
↳ *Your note ("what? how was I supposed to make this connection? what did I miss?"):* this wasn't outside knowledge — it's a **two-sentence textual link** across paragraphs:
- Para 2: "These toxins were **identified** and used to create a representative **'conditioned' medium**."
- Para 3: "Two **histone proteins (H1 and H2B) were included in the conditioned medium**."
- ⇒ The identified neurotoxic factors **are** the histones. Then normal-function knowledge finishes it: histones bind/stabilize **DNA** → Q22 answer = DNA; and histones live in the **nucleus = soma** → Q21 answer = soma.

**The "representative" trap (what actually threw you):** you read "a *representative* 'conditioned' medium" as *"the medium only stands in for the toxin, so it's definitely NOT the toxin."* That attached "representative" to the wrong noun. Grammar: "these toxins **were identified** and **used to create**" the medium → the toxins are *put into* it. **"Representative" describes the MEDIUM**, not the histones — a "conditioned medium" is medium that held cells and now carries their secreted factors, and a *representative* one is a **rebuilt, defined version** that faithfully recreates the original toxic soup *because it contains the real identified toxins*. So "representative" means "a recreated stand-in for the original medium," not "a fake without the real toxin."

**The backstop that makes the word irrelevant — you had THREE confirmations:** even if "representative" stayed ambiguous, the passage told you H1 is neurotoxic in three separate places:
1. The para 2→3 link — the identified toxins are the histones in the conditioned medium.
2. **Figure 1 (Panel A)** — purified **H1** alone raises apoptotic protease release in neurons → H1 *is* demonstrably toxic, not merely "representing" a toxin.
3. The **final paragraph**, almost explicitly: *"Histone H1 released from dying neurons **causes death of neighboring neurons**..."*

So this wasn't a subtle single-clue inference — three independent reads all confirm H1 = neurotoxic factor. You didn't need to win the grammar fight over "representative"; either the figure or the last paragraph alone closes it.

*MCAT relevance / pattern:* this is the **"use ALL the passage"** habit again (cf. Q16, Q18). Two sub-lessons: (1) when a question says "the factor/toxin/compound described," trace back to what it was *identified as* before answering; (2) **when one sentence is ambiguous, don't let a single word anchor you — check whether the data/figures resolve it.** Here an ambiguous word ("representative") talked you *out* of a connection Figure 1 would have confirmed. Also the AAMC trick: the histones act as **toxins** in the experiment, but the questions reward their **normal** role (bind DNA in the nucleus) — don't let the unusual passage role overwrite the textbook function.
