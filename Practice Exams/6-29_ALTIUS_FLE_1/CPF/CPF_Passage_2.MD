## Passage 2 (Questions 5–9)

Biological buffers maintain the pH of specific fluid compartments within organ systems and cells. An ideal biological buffer has two defining qualities. The buffer must function in a fluid compartment with a pH within one pKₐ unit of the buffering agent, and it must not react with other biomolecules within that compartment.

Blood plasma is regulated by the bicarbonate buffering system shown in equations 1 and 2.

**Equation 1:**   CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺   (pKₐ at 25 °C = 6.1)

**Equation 2:**   CO₂ + H₂O ↔ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺

When the bloodstream becomes too acidic or too basic, the organism employs compensatory mechanisms to restore equilibrium.

A chemist was investigating the buffering properties of a bicarbonate solution in the lab. The investigator began with a 4.3 g sample of crotonic acid (CH₃CHCHCOOH, pKₐ at 25 °C = 6.1) dissolved in 100 mL of distilled water. The resulting solution was added dropwise to a bicarbonate solution and pH was monitored using a pH meter.

## Questions

### Question 5 ✗ (missed)

Suppose a student were to make a bicarbonate solution buffered to a pH of 7.1. What would be the approximate ratio of [H₂CO₃] to [HCO₃⁻] in the solution?

- *Subtopic 655 — Equilibrium constants Ka and Kb: pKa' pKb · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 1: Knowledge of Scientific Concepts and Principles · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. 1:100
- B. 10:1 — **✗ (her answer)**
- **C. 1:10 ✓ (correct)**
- D. 1:20

**Solution:** Use Henderson–Hasselbalch. The pKₐ of the bicarbonate system is 6.1. Using pH = pKₐ − log([HA]/[A⁻]): 7.1 = 6.1 − log([HA]/[A⁻]) → 1.0 = −log([HA]/[A⁻]) → [HA]/[A⁻] = 10⁻¹ = **0.1 = 1:10 (Choice C).** Conceptual shortcut: for every 1.0 unit the pH sits *above* the pKₐ, conjugate base outnumbers acid by a factor of 10. Since pH (7.1) > pKₐ (6.1) by exactly one unit, there is 10× more HCO₃⁻ than H₂CO₃ — i.e., acid:base = 1:10. **Her trap (B, 10:1):** that's the inverse — it would be right only if you used the pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻]/[HA]) form and forgot the question asked acid-to-base, not base-to-acid. *Externalize which species the stem wants in the numerator before computing.*

### Question 6

Patients with Type I Diabetes can develop blood ketoacidosis due to the excessive breakdown of fatty acids. What effect does this increase in acid concentration have on blood pH during ketoacidosis?

- *Subtopic 1073 — Buffers (definition and concepts [common buffer systems], influence on titration curves) · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. Low pH, shifting Equation 2 to the right
- **B. Low pH, shifting Equation 2 to the left ✓ (correct — her answer)**
- C. High pH, shifting Equation 2 to the right
- D. High pH, shifting Equation 2 to the left

**Solution:** High ketone bodies → keto acids → ketoacidosis. The added acid means more H⁺ (lower pH) and shifts the buffering system in Equation 2 to the **left** (Le Chatelier), converting HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ back toward CO₂. A is wrong given that leftward shift. C and D are wrong because blood becomes *more* acidic, so pH must go *down*, not up.

### Question 7

Hyperventilation (rapid breathing) occurs in response to respiratory acidosis because hyperventilation:

- *Subtopic 1073 — Buffers... · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- **A. increases blood pH by decreasing CO₂ concentrations. ✓ (correct — her answer)**
- B. increases blood pH by decreasing HCO₃⁻ concentrations.
- C. decreases blood pH by decreasing CO₂ concentrations.
- D. decreases blood pH by decreasing HCO₃⁻ concentrations.

**Solution:** Rapid breathing removes CO₂ faster than it is produced, shifting Equation 2 to the **left** (CO₂ + H₂O ← HCO₃⁻ + H⁺), consuming H⁺ and *raising* blood pH. B is wrong — decreasing HCO₃⁻ would lower pH, not raise it. C is wrong — hyperventilation increases pH. D is wrong — hyperventilation does not regulate HCO₃⁻.

### Question 8

Pearl divers in Japan are able to dive for long periods of time, without breathing, to harvest pearls. Is it reasonable to conclude that the blood pH of these divers would decrease near the end of a dive?

- *Subtopic 1073 — Buffers... · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. No, because the divers will be functioning anaerobically and therefore not producing CO₂.
- B. No, because accumulated CO₂ will be diffused from the diver, preventing blood acidosis.
- C. Yes, because the diver will use accumulated CO₂ for glucose production to fuel muscle contraction.
- **D. Yes, because accumulated CO₂ will build up in the bloodstream, resulting in acidosis. ✓ (correct — her answer)**

**Solution:** Without the ability to inhale and exhale, CO₂ gradually builds up in the blood, shifting Equation 2 to the right (Le Chatelier) → acidosis. A is wrong — CO₂ still accumulates in muscles even under anaerobic conditions. B is wrong — there is no way to exhale the CO₂ during the dive. C is wrong — CO₂ used by other metabolic pathways would not consume enough to prevent acidosis.

### Question 9 ✗ (missed)

H₃PO₄, the molecule primarily responsible for the maintenance of pH in the cytosol, contains three acidic protons. Which equilibrium constant is associated with cytosolic buffering by H₃PO₄?

*(A titration curve of pH vs. equivalents of OH⁻ is shown, with three horizontal buffering plateaus centered near pH 2, pH 7, and pH 13.)*

- *Subtopic 655 — Equilibrium constants Ka and Kb: pKa' pKb · Topic 172 — Acid; Base Equilibria · SIR 2: Scientific Reasoning and Problem Solving · Discipline 4: General Chemistry*

- A. [H₂PO₄⁻]/[HPO₄²⁻][H⁺]
- **B. [HPO₄²⁻][H⁺]/[H₂PO₄⁻] ✓ (correct)**
- C. [PO₄³⁻][H⁺]/[HPO₄²⁻]
- D. [H₂PO₄⁻][H⁺]/[H₃PO₄] — **✗ (her answer)**

**Solution:** The phosphate system loses three protons (H₃PO₄ → PO₄³⁻), each dissociation shown as one of the three flat segments on the titration curve. The center of each flat segment is a half-equivalence point (pH = pKₐ). Cytosolic pH ≈ 7.0, which matches the **middle** plateau — the **second** dissociation: H₂PO₄⁻ ⇌ HPO₄²⁻ + H⁺, whose Kₐ = **[HPO₄²⁻][H⁺]/[H₂PO₄⁻] (Choice B).** **Her trap (D):** D is the *first* dissociation (of H₃PO₄, centered near pH 2 — far too acidic for cytosol). C is the *third* (near pH 13 — far too basic). A is just B with numerator and denominator switched (equilibrium expressions are products-over-reactants). *This is a "match the pH to the right plateau" problem — pin cytosol at pH 7 → middle proton.*
